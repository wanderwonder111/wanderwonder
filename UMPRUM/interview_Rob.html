Could you briefly tell us about the Latitude Longitude and how the collaboration between two universities started?

RVLLatitude Longitude is a collaborative workshop between UMPRUM in Prague and our department at HEAD Geneva bringing together 44 Graphic design students. The project unfolds in two chapters in Mount Sněžka and the River Saône. I came into it a little later than my counterparts in Prague. At that point, the framework had already been set up to some extent, although the details still had to be decided. The first chapter was held in the Czech Republic, where Zuzana Lednická and Richard Jaroš were in charge of organizing and defining this initial phase.

To give a bit of background, we have a partnership with UMPRUM, and a couple of years ago a delegation from our Visual Communication department went to Prague to meet people there and discuss future projects. This workshop is one of the results of that partnership, supported by European funding. It was decided to run the workshops outside the two cities — Prague and Geneva — mainly to foster connections between students through a rather isolated environment, and to propose a more analog way of working, as a way of stepping back from our daily digital workspaces. Also, we wanted to encourage more experimental ways of creating content, and also confront the students with a new natural environment.

How did you decide on the two locations — Mount Sněžka and the River Saône?

RVLThe location in the Czech Republic was decided by Zuzana and Richard. They really took the initiative and picked a great environment because it's a national park with beautiful surroundings. We were there in early November, off-season, so it was calm and isolated even though it's quite a touristic area in winter for skiing and in summer for hiking. The mountain trail runs along the Czech-Polish border, which also added an interesting dimension in terms of identity and territory. Besides the main hike that we did at the start of the week, we were able to go on walks and hikes in the surrounding area, and it was really nice to be out there. Mount Sněžka became the protagonist of the first chapter.

For the second chapter in France, we had to come up with a counterpart. At HEAD, our department usually goes to Fleurville every September for a workshop with the new first-year bachelor students. They go there for a week to get to know each other and to start their academic career. It's an ancient eau-de-vie distillery that has been running artistic workshops for 25 years, and it really suits large groups for artistic work and workshops. It's situated on the border of the Saône. So it's a large river, a big site, with plenty of natural habitats around. It's a protected area right in the middle of the Burgundy region. There's a lot of cultural heritage, a lot of vineyards around. This place suits perfectly for big groups and for artistic work because it's really an inspiring place. So this river became the protagonist of the second workshop.

We will do a kayak excursion to explore the river not just from the shore but from within — a first day of encounters to really observe the place and to kind of immerse ourselves in this area.

What approaches did you use during the first workshop in the Czech Republic?

RVLWe really left that up to the students to explore what they wanted—whether they wanted to stay within what they already know and develop that further, or if they wanted to experiment with different mediums that they haven't mastered yet. We had a wide variety of backgrounds in the Czech Republic, even if we were all somehow related to graphic design studies. There were illustrators, photographers, more digitally oriented profiles, and so on. And because they had to work in groups, the question became: what is the common demeanor of the approach that becomes something really fluid and is decided in the moment. We tried to guide the groups as much as possible so that every member could have an impact and contribute to the collective work.

You brought tools like printers and scanners from the school environment, how did you decide to bring them to the mountain?

RVLThere was a really practical setup from UMPRUM. They organized a bus to go there, and one of the teachers, Richard, brought all the material by car. They listed all the tools they wanted to bring for the students beforehand, and basically moved everything there. The tools were quite diverse and intuitive. Quite analog at first, collecting material along the way.

Obviously, digital tools were not excluded entirely; they were still used for documentation and analysis. Maybe our students were less prepared, we knew that we were going to do a hike, but we didn't know it would be that long or exhausting. We were hiking along the Polish-Czech border to an altitude of around 1400 to 1500 meters. It was quite an intense first day, but it really helped to bring people together and to get to know each other. At the end of the day, we created mixed teams between the Czech and Geneva students to work together through the rest of the week.

Could you tell us about the final outcomes?

RVLThe outcome was set to be posters, but let's say "posters" was a very broadly interpreted definition. We printed in black and white, and the format wasn't strictly defined, it just had to be printable on a plotter of about 90 centimeters width. There was one group that did a huge roll of several meters. The space where we exhibited the results was kind of a cave-like gallery environment where we could really spread those rolls out however we wanted. We wanted to leave the outcome as free as possible. As long as it could be printed, it was fine.

Were there any unexpected discoveries during this collaboration, or in the students' work process?

RVLWe went there pretty open-minded. Speaking for myself and my colleagues, I don't know what the students expected from it. That's maybe something you should ask them yourselves.

What the week offered was a kind of reflection on our own ways of working in Geneva. I think the Czech students were more in charge of the workshop, because they were "at home" and better prepared than we were, so they took the lead, which was great because we came as the visitors this time. Our students also experienced this, and reflected on finding themselves more in the following role. This also translated into group discussions, decisions to be made, and things to be prepared. The Czech students were constantly taking the lead, and at some point that created — not friction exactly — but a recognition that our students should maybe step in more, or be more vocal and more present in the process. This also came up in project discussion, sometimes, out of habit, things were discussed in Czech rather than English. So we had to stress to remind everyone "OK, let's speak English." It's something that happens naturally, and you have to be aware of it and defend your own position within it. That awareness is also necessary for creating collectively, rather than it becoming an individual endeavor again.

From your perspective as an educator, what can students gain from moving or changing location?

RVLIt's really about putting things into perspective. It's always great as a student to be able to work in a different environment. It makes you think, it makes you slow down, and it offers you a different perspective, almost as if you look at your own studies from the outside. I think that's really helpful at certain points. Our semesters are also organized with that in mind—we always have a workshop in between the courses. Then, of course, encountering other forms of creation, other identities, and habits from different countries or cultures—that's also super rich and inspiring, obviously.

We also saw on the website that you had institutional support from the Swiss Embassy and your school. How did this expand the possibilities for this kind of experimental class?

RVLThe Swiss Embassy was very interested in joining and participating, to encourage any Swiss activity on Czech soil and to be part of the encounter between the students and the team. They kindly organized drinks on the opening evening, and the teaching staff was also invited to dinner at the Ambassador's residency. I think this brings things to a more meta level of diplomacy, relations, and international exchange. We were very grateful that they wanted to participate, which is not very common actually.

So this was more of an addition or a plus, without it, the workshop wouldn't have been fundamentally different. But institutions are necessary to make such experiences possible, because there is money involved, as well as infrastructure and teaching staff. All of this has to be financed. So without these more traditional structures, this kind of experimental way of working wouldn't be possible— at least not with the means we had available.

For the final question: we see many institutions increasingly holding classes outside the traditional environment. Do you think we will still need architecture for design schools in the future?

RVLI see around me that there are other ways of educating today—more online tools, more independent educators offering classes. I think this enriches the overall offer, and I don't see it as a negative development.

The institution still remains relevant in a physical sense. We are finally getting out of the COVID slump, when creating became an almost solely individual endeavor, and we've seen the limits of that in the years since. You learn by exchange, and a physical space enables that. A classroom allows students to work together and collaborate. Students often learn as much, if not more, from each other than from teachers. We set up the framework, reflect with them, help and guide them, but we are not present on a day-to-day basis. What happens daily is up to the students themselves, and they rely on other resources, whether peers or online sources. I would say this is essential to any creative process and very healthy.

In that sense, things are evolving, hopefully in parallel, without one replacing the other. Schools today have to position themselves more precisely than before, though. We also see that enrollment levels vary, partly because there are other ways of learning graphic design now. Whether graphic design remains an attractive profession is also an open question. These are all factors that institutions need to consider.
