The Dutch Art Institute is quite unique in that the school itself is fully nomadic. Its manifesto states, “Farewell bricks, farewell walls, mobilize our bodies, our intelligences.” Could you briefly introduce what it means in the context of DAI?

ZLFrom my understanding, DAI functions under the Dutch art education system. It's part of a bigger university program called the ArtEZ University of Arts based in Arnhem, the Netherlands. Within this, there are different master’s programs, and I think the two special ones are Werkplaats Typografie and Dutch Art Institute.

Before, DAI had a campus in Enschede, a city close to Germany on the east side of the Netherlands, where they shared a building with another program called AKI — also an art school. Around 2016-2017, they decided to fully transition to roaming teaching. But before that, they already had some roaming programs. Once or twice a year, they traveled to other parts of Europe or even further: New York, China, India. I think this very much reflects the current director’s approach to art education.

One year they went to India, to the West Bengal region where there's a school called Shantaniketan. It's an art program founded by the famous Bengali poet Rabindranath Tagore. It was an example and introduction for DAI to think about alternative ways of teaching and learning art, not only through the curriculum, courses or texts, but also through a sense of living.

From 2017, DAI started roaming. The school receives funding from the education department, and renting a building was taking up a large part of it, so they started thinking about alternative ways of using these resources more effectively. They decided to give up the campus and start roaming. Many roaming academies, for example, School of the Damned in the UK — do not offer a master's degree, while DAI is still finding a balance within the Dutch system while also staying creative within that framework.

How did you find the school?

ZLI found it through friends. I was studying in Chicago and one of my friends was planning to move to Europe. She studied at Werkplaats Typografie, and she told me about other programs. I looked it up and it sounded interesting. Relocating from China to the US, I wanted to see more of Europe next, not just the Netherlands, and DAI seemed like the perfect fit.

Without any centralized physical building, how do the classes and overall curriculum actually work?

ZLBasically, all our students are based in different parts of Europe. I think maybe one fifth or one sixth of us are living in the Netherlands. Everyone else is in the EU or other countries, like Turkey and Australia.

How many students are there?

ZLNow we have around 75 students in total across the two-year master program. I think we are the largest master's program in arts in the Netherlands.

Probably it's also a lot because there is no fixed building.

ZLEvery year we have around seven meetings and we call confluence. Each confluence takes place in a different location, and everyone flies in from wherever they're. Usually it's between 10 days and two weeks, and within that time we study, eat, and sleep together — it's a very close community environment.

The school is divided into four parts or columns. The first one is called How to Do Things with Theory, which is our theory class. The second one is Co-op — cooperation which I find really interesting and quite different from other programs. Co-op is where the school cooperates with other art institutions in Europe. For example, Archive Book in Milan, Savvy Contemporary in Berlin, De Appel in Amsterdam, and If I Can't Dance, also from Amsterdam. Basically, students are divided into six groups, and each group works with a different art institution. These are institutions already operating in the art field, and each year they send visiting tutors to the school. Every confluence we have two or three days of seminars or workshops with them. Toward the end of the year there's a Co-op Summit, where each group organizes a public event.

The third column is Kitchen — a space for each student to present and develop their own practice. Every year you have 20 minutes to show what you've been working on — a performance, a film, a research project, some writing, anything.

The fourth column is Factory, which is a student-led event. During each confluence, students have one or two days free to organize their own events: workshops, walks, bird watching, all kinds of things, whatever you can imagine.

Before each confluence, how do you normally communicate with your classmates?

ZLOfficial announcements are sent through email, and we have a group chat. But even the official meetings are flexible, the school is very open in that sense. Students also have a lot of say in how things run. If something isn't working, students organize a time to meet with the school and discuss it together. It's a very flexible, different kind of structure.

Could you give us an example of something that didn't work well for students?

ZLBecause we're always roaming, accommodation takes a lot of time and discussion, we have to decide who we're sharing a room with. In my first year we just had one big Excel form for every meeting. For 75 people, there would be a list of all the rooms, some for one person, some for two, some for three or four. Then you had to wait for the form to be released, then go in and put your name in the room that you wanted. A lot of people felt it didn't work very well. It was very stressful. So we voted in our telegram group to have one person to organize it instead. Now students fill in little preference forms — things like "I can't sleep with someone who snores" or "I can't sleep with someone who wakes up early." Now there's a student volunteer who manages it, and it became a self-organized system, because it's very much about holding the community together.

Do you have a budget for accommodation or do you need to pay every time?

ZLAccommodation is covered by the school through Bread and Bed Solidarity Fund. In the Netherlands, non-EU students pay full tuition — roughly ten times what an EU student pays. So at DAI, EU students pay a little extra on top of their tuition, and that money is pooled collectively to cover accommodation and travel. Almost every time we travel, we get half of our flight refunded.

Who runs the school? Do you have professors?

ZLIt's a big team. There are a lot of people working on logistics — booking hotels, buses and all the classrooms. That's a big part of how the school operates. We also have professors, or theory tutors, who mostly work part-time. Many of them teach at other universities in Berlin, London, Athens, or the US, and fly in for each confluence. That travel is also covered by the school. So most people at DAI work part-time, including the logistics team.

Where are you currently based, and what do you work on?

ZLI live in Amsterdam, which is the most convenient location for non-EU students, because the school is still within the Dutch educational system. To get a student visa, I have to prove that I'm based in the Netherlands.

Between school sessions I work on my own practice. I used to have a studio and work part-time, but now I mostly work with analog film at a film lab in Rotterdam. Recently, since I'm graduating, I've had to focus on my graduation work and thesis, so I stopped my part-time job to fully focus on writing and making.

For your thesis or other work at DAI, do you work with physical or digital outputs? Could you tell us about your practice and research process?

ZLBecause of the format and the political agenda of the program, I think the students somehow have some similarities. We don't have so many painters or sculptors, because you're always traveling and the only thing you carry is a suitcase. So there is this idea: how do you show your work just with a suitcase?

Because of that, we have a lot of performance artists, people who work with movement like dancers, writers, curators, and theorists. There is a small group of filmmakers; I'm one of the people who work with images. This kind of medium is just easier to travel with.

We only have two presentations per year. Out of our seven confluences, five of them don't require a formal presentation, so you can just travel there with your clothes and a notebook. It's only during those two presentation times that people bring extra luggage with their materials. Otherwise, a lot of people just improvise with what they can find in the location.

As we know the school supports the relationship between alumni and current students. Could you share more on how that works in practice?

ZLI think it happens very organically. I haven't graduated yet, so I don't know the full picture, but I've met some DAI alumni through some events. For instance, many of our part-time tutors are DAI graduates. The school regularly invites alumni who are now established artists, writers, and curators to return as guests and provide feedback on our student presentations. The Netherlands is a small country, there's a large group of DAI alumni in Rotterdam, and people just naturally run into each other.

Also, in recent years, we've had a program called the Rojava/Palestine Teach-Out, which responds to the current political situation and invites artists and theorists from that background. Many of them are DAI alumni returning to the school.

You mentioned it briefly, but what does mentoring look like in practice? How do the tutors actually work with you and give feedback?

ZLMentorship is a bit more difficult in this format. When we're writing our theory or working on our presentations, we're only together with our tutors for two or three days. Those days are very short for things to develop. In a creative process, thoughts take time, it happens momentarily. In a traditional setting with a campus, you might have a random thought over the weekend, and just go to school on Monday to talk with your tutor and discuss it. With DAI's format, it's a bit more difficult. Our tutors are usually busy with other work, many of them teaching elsewhere or have full-time jobs, and they do this as a part-time position. However, at DAI, you build personal relationships with these people. Now I'm close enough with some of my tutors that when I have a thought, I can just email them or send them a text over Telegram or WhatsApp. Because we're not in a strict institutional environment, the relationship naturally becomes more personal.

DAI positions itself with a feminist, intersectional, anti-capitalist, and decolonial orientation, driven by a 'queering spirit.' How does operating without a permanent building relate to these core political and social philosophies?

ZLEvery year, we organize a public program called the Roaming Assembly, which is a one-day conference or art event. We invite local artists, activists, and cultural workers to give talks, run seminars, or join discussions with the students. For instance, last year, we were in Nicosia, Cyprus, hosted by NiMAC — the State Gallery of Contemporary Art.

Our time there was deeply connected to its geopolitical situation. Cyprus is an island divided into two parts, and it still maintains a border within the island itself, sitting right on the edge of the EU. While we were there, we had a lot of discussions about what is happening in Cyprus, approaching its history through the lens of British colonialism and other regional complexities.

Does the director always pick the locations, or do the students get a say?

ZLI think it's always decided by the school. But sometimes it happens through a tutor's recommendation. Last year we went to Cyprus because we have a tutor who is from there. She suggested going there, and because she already had strong personal connections with the local institutions, it turned into a collaborative project. That's usually how it works, but I don't think the students participate in choosing the locations that much.

As a final question, how do you imagine what will be the role of design education in the future? And do you think we still need architecture for our future design schools?

ZLThat's such a big question! I think for some people, the idea of a roaming academy is enough—or even more interesting than working in one static place—because communication is so easy now. In general, Europe has a lot of public infrastructure and public spaces you can access, which provide extra support outside of a traditional art academy.

But it really depends on the stage of the artist or designer. For people who are already somewhat established, have a stable workflow, or a dedicated studio space, this format is incredibly interesting. It provides an opportunity to continue your education without pausing what's already happening in your current city or professional setting.

However, for younger students, maybe they still need that physical environment. So much of education doesn't just happen within the classroom, but inside the buildings or simply on campus. At DAI, the average age of our students is around 33 or 34, which is older than a regular master's program. Everyone already has an active practice, and they are doing this program as a kind of 'side quest.' So for this demographic, a physical building isn't necessary, but for others, it might be.
