Could you briefly introduce the course “History of Images and Media”?

ITHSince the very beginning of the MA course at our institute in 2009/10, “History of Images and Media” (Lecture / Seminar) has been an integral part of the two-year MA course. The main purpose is to provide students with basic knowledge and experience regarding crucial perceptive and pictorial developments in the diverse field of visual media, starting in the late Middle Ages and early Renaissance—the shift when panel painting on canvas instead of wooden carriers became increasingly fashionable, making images more portable, and when illuminated book illustration was progressively replaced by printed books (Gutenberg’s letterpress; using paper instead of parchment, etc.).

What led you to move this class outside the classroom? How do Basel and its cultural environment influence the development of this format?

ITHSimply because nothing—no analog or digital reproduction technique—can replace discussing pictorial and technical issues and visual phenomena in front of original artworks, whether it is an oil painting or a silkscreen poster. And I think experiencing such phenomena in front of the original is also a much more lasting and clarifying experience. Basel is blessed with a great variety of cultural institutions like museums, archives, and galleries, and it would be ignorant and a missed opportunity if I did not show and introduce this cultural environment to the students.

Since exhibitions constantly change, how does this fluid setup affect the way you design the course compared to a fixed curriculum?

ITHA couple of years ago, instead of following a strict chronological and systematic order throughout the semester, I decided to visit special exhibitions when they take place and/or make archives and collections accessible to students that are not open to the public right now. I am aware that—especially at the beginning of a semester/study—this can cause confusion and irritation. But after a few weeks, students should get conditioned to this kind of exercise because it’s all about visual phenomena, only different in time, style, and technique. We are not talking about quantum physics.

For a better understanding of historical developments and how things are interlocked together over the centuries, students receive—at least to my understanding—more than sufficient additional information/material in the form of text and image files, bibliographic and theoretical recommendations, etc. This material is left for self-study. It’s just one semester and the schedule is really tight. If a student has no time or energy to go through these files, they have all the necessary material and references accessible at any time in the future.

This, of course, can also be perceived by some students as overwhelming, and maybe some might regard this procedure as didactically and pedagogically bad, but it’s an MA course and not a weekend party, and I think from MA students one should expect a certain level of independent initiative and interest in such issues. Of course, if students have specific questions/requests, they can ask and get support.

Looking back at this experience, how do you think this kind of alternative learning environment might influence students’ design practice?

ITHI don’t think I can give you a cohesive answer which includes all students, because it’s very individual what students do / filter out from each lesson. Each one is interested in different issues, some more, some less. It’s a lesson within the Visual Communication Institute and maybe some students expect design-related issues only.

But because many visual and pictorial phenomena we find in (graphic) design were originally invented in the field of art (design as an individual term is a rather young discipline), and due to the fact that it becomes more and more difficult to separate the different media from each other (which does not make sense either, because many things are tightly connected with each other), I think this lesson can set free many valuable triggers/ideas in each student’s own imagination/specific interest to help students find their own way through the thicket of infinite possibilities.

As a final question: do you think we will still need architecture for the future design school?

ITHFor a couple of years during the COVID-pandemic, within one week we had to shift physical lessons to virtual/digital ones—so-called distance learning. It was a huge challenge for both sides, students as well as teachers. Emotionally and psychologically, it was a difficult time. Ask students who experienced this kind of teaching! When we were allowed to return to physical lessons either in seminar or exhibition rooms, it was regarded as a huge relief by both sides, and returning to physical classes also had a much more positive effect on the classes themselves, respectively the students, who again had the possibility to meet in person and to exchange ideas.

Therefore, yes, we need appropriate and convenient rooms/architecture for this kind of (human) interaction and exchange. Online/digital tools are great if there is no other alternative possible at the moment or to bridge spatial distance for lectures, etc., to make an event happen at least in digital form, but it can’t replace real teaching/learning in physical surroundings.
