JKWe had a class “History of Images and Media” together — a class where we visited different museums across Basel each week to observe and discuss works and exhibitions. What was it like?

AAIt happened every Wednesday afternoon, and almost every time we had to cut into our lunch break to get to the venue. We were always a little late. But it was great that Invar organised every visit and made sure someone was always waiting for us there.

ATIt was also quite exciting because at the beginning of the semester, we didn't know the full schedule. We only knew the next five lectures ahead — what they'd cover and where we'd go — but we had no idea about the rest. So it was always a surprise. It was also our first time in Basel, so it was a chance to explore the museums too.

VMCI remember always being in a rush for this class. It felt like you were never going to make it on time. The museums aren't all in the city centre — some are on the outskirts — and our school isn't centrally located either. So it was like a mission: first, to get there on time, and then to cool down and shift into the right mindset to look at an exhibition and be present enough to observe, analyse, and reflect on the images.

ATHow was your experience when you first arrived in Basel, especially with this course being part of your schedule? Because for me, when I arrived in a new city, I immediately started my studies and I really didn’t have any time to explore the city. But I felt relieved, I didn’t have time to go around the city, but at least it was part of my schedule.

AAYeah, same for me. When I arrived in Basel, I knew that it was a very important place in terms of art history and Swiss culture. Personally, it was hard to find the local knowledge on my own, I knew that there was a Kunstmuseum, and I visited it before classes began. But as we started to explore more, visiting locations that aren’t on the typical TripAdvisor list, it became much more valuable. Then you become more local, you integrate into the culture, the sphere, the area where you’re supposed to work. As an introduction to both studies and to city life in a place where I’d never lived before, it was very meaningful.

VMCI came to Basel twice in August doing my moving and I think I wanted to reserve the museum part. I was saving the best for later, because I walked through the city and just wandered around. I was tempted to go to the Kunstmuseum but told myself, “okay, I will find another time.” First I wanted to get a feel for the streets and the city itself first. But also I remember there was a Kunsttage in August with a lot of openings in the city so I started visiting galleries instead of museums.

JKI was surprised by how many museums there are here. When I first moved, the city was so small compared to Seoul, where I lived before. You can walk across the whole city. But we have so many museums, even more than we visited during the class.

VMCYes, Basel is one of the cities with the highest concentration of museums per inhabitant in Europe. So when I arrived, I had high expectations about that particular aspect.

JKWhen I travelled in Europe before, I saw a lot of students in museums, sitting on the floor, discussing works. I was kind of jealous. That's not really part of my background in Korean culture. So when I heard we'd have this kind of class, I was so excited. I still remember posting about it on instagram after the first session, saying something like, “this was on my bucket list.”

ATDid any of you have similar courses in your previous studies?

AAI only had one, but it wasn’t similar. It was a history of British art course during my bachelor’s studies, and we had one visit to the Hermitage collection in St. Petersburg, where they have a whole section of British works. I would never have explored it on my own, but it gave me a chance to get an overview of those works in the museum. I also had to write an essay about one of the works found there.

ATI also didn’t have regular weekly classes in museums. But we always had spontaneous meetings whenever there was a new exhibition opening or an interesting cultural event happening in Tbilisi. It was never planned, the head of the program would just post in a Facebook group, “There is a new exhibition from this artist, let’s meet there.” And sometimes, instead of regular studio time, we’d have these gallery visits.

VMCIn Madrid, we didn’t have regular meetings either. But teachers used to recommend things like, “Hey, you have to go to this exhibition,” or, “For this exhibition, you should go and write an essay about it.” So, we always ended up going to the exhibitions with the excuse of an assignment — just for them to make sure we were attending. But for me, it wasn’t necessary, because I was going to all of them whenever I had the chance. Also, being a student means you get in for free — which is one of the best things about being a student, getting access to all these amazing places that usually cost a lot.

ATWhat did you think about Invar’s approach during these museum visits? We had maybe an hour to look around by ourselves, and then come together for a group discussion.

AAI think it’s a good approach. I liked it much more than the first day of just being talked to, because I don’t really enjoy guided tours. When I visit a museum or a gallery, I want to form my own opinions and impressions. Exploring the space and the works by yourself means you can spend as much time as you need in front of each work. If something doesn’t catch your attention, you just move on.

I remember one of my favourite moments was the Tinguely Museum during Julian Charrière’s exhibition. It was really important to be alone, because some of the rooms were dark and quiet with the works as the only source of light. It was very dim. People weren’t spending much time in those rooms, but I stayed for a long time just experiencing that moment of silence and darkness — just me and the works. It was really cool.

VMCI remember that day, we arrived almost at the same time. We were so late. I remember running across the bridge on foot.

AAI got lost on the way. I was biking there and got confused trying to find the right bridge.

JKTonya and Victor, you were always the ones actively debating and discussing in class. But you were always late, and Invar was always looking for you.

I really enjoyed the exhibition. The quality was great, and it was quite large. There was a meditation room, and we also gathered in a light room where we experienced differences in light, which was unexpected. I thought it was a really well-made exhibition. But when we discussed it, Invar and one of our classmates pointed out that it was a good exhibition to experience, but not necessarily good art. After the discussion, my perspective kind of shifted, because I’d never thought about it from that angle before. Did any of you have this kind of experience during our class, where a discussion changed how you saw something?

AAI think my impression of the works didn’t really change. For me, experiencing the work in person was more valuable than just reading an article saying that some money was spent on environmental problems. I didn’t think much about ecology during that experience itself, even though it was one of the topics. Maybe I’m just not that engaged with the issue. But experiencing art is something that I value deeply.

ATHow do you think being physically present in exhibitions affected our discussion or interpretation of these images?

VMCThis might sound contradictory, but I felt more focused during class. When I’m in an exhibition, I’m more aware of myself — my body and my presence in the room. I pay more attention to the people around me and the space itself. Because there is less stimulation, your relation to the work changes. In museums there are also more elements involved, like other works around you, and which makes a big difference.

AAYes, for example, it was super crowded at the Beyeler Foundation during Yayoi Kusama’s exhibition. Sometimes these experiences were difficult, but I still think it is important to see, especially graphic works in real life. Kusama used very bright, fluorescent colors on black paper or a black background, creating the impression that the work itself was a source of light. For me, her early works were especially interesting because of the materiality and the way she works with color. It was very interesting to observe them closely, we would never experience the same thing just by looking at them on a screen.

Also, an immersive experience like the one at the Tinguely Museum couldn’t be recreated in a classroom. In the last two sessions, we discussed Warhol more theoretically in the classroom. You learn about approaches, history, and cultural context, but you don’t experience the work in the way it’s meant to be experienced. I don’t think that discussing works only through projections or screens is very productive. And when people are sitting in a classroom in front of their laptops, they’re just not very motivated to participate in the discussion.

JKMuseums always try to create the best conditions to experience the work — lighting, air, sound, and everything. Also, you can get physically closer to the work, which is completely different from zooming in on a screen where the resolution is fixed. I was often surprised — for example, Tonya pointed out very small details that I’d never noticed, and then I could move closer and see them myself. The group discussions in the exhibition space were really impressive for that reason. But on a screen, it’s much harder to discuss things together.

VMCYeah, it also depends on the format. For video works, the classroom environment actually helped. But for something like Andy Warhol’s images, it’s definitely much better to see them in person. The format of the work is really key.

ATI think it’s also a case of installations. I had seen many of Kusama’s installations online, and my perception of the materials was completely different. They looked very different from what I had imagined and how I had perceived them on the screen.

JKIt’s kind of a fun question — if you had to design your own class in Basel, which museum or exhibition would you choose?

I’d go to the paper museum. We never went during our class, but as a graphic design department, it would make a lot of sense. They have a letterpress — it was actually a bit of a surprise that we don’t have one at our school. They also have incredible resources, like original Helvetica and other typefaces, and people who actually draw typefaces. As graphic design students, it would be a really valuable experience.

AAFor me, the most valuable experience — and one I don’t think I’ll ever have again — was visiting the storage at Schaulager. If I were organizing a class like this, I’d focus on that kind of access: giving students the opportunity to visit storage spaces, because it’s completely different from seeing a regular exhibition.

JKAs a final question, how do you imagine the future of design education? Do you think we still need architecture?

VMCI think architecture will always be needed, especially for education. Architecture has to adapt to education, but education also needs a physical environment. It doesn’t need to be fixed, but it has to be prepared for study, to gather, to have conversations and discussions. The environment is key.

I remember some of the locations where we had this class weren’t really appropriate. There was no sound quality — just echoes and noise from people around us. There was no space to sit. All of these elements should be taken into consideration to create a good education system, ecosystem. So architecture is key, I think.

AAOf course, it’s very important how you experience that material, and it sets the whole studying environment. When we visited Amsterdam and stayed in a hostel, sometimes we didn’t sleep very well. It affected how we experienced the visits and how we felt during the day. If you organize this kind of nomadic class, it’s important to create the right conditions for a proper experience. Also as we mentioned earlier, the constant rush also affected the whole experience a lot.

VMCIt’s those little elements that people sometimes overlook, but which actually matter a great deal.

AAMaybe in the future there’ll be more awareness about this, and nomadic classes will be better organized. But overall, I think there should be more of this kind of learning, especially in humanities, and maybe even in other fields. If you’re an engineer and visiting a production line and seeing how things actually really work, you have both a practical element and a change of environment. That shift in context makes you more attentive, and maybe the learning outcome is better because of it.

JKIn Korea, we have a saying — “Seeing once is better than hearing a hundred times(백문이 불여일견).” So for this kind of art class, I think being outside, in the real space, will always be worth more than any classroom.
