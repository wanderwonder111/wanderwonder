Could you tell us about Stand-in School and how it is integrated in the university schedule?

ATAt the Graphic Design MA at EKA, travel is a huge part of the curriculum. Our class was the first time Stand-in School happened. Sean Yendris and Alex proposed the idea: we would organize a public school, open to everyone, invite people to run workshops and lectures, and do the same ourselves. It is public-facing and focuses on engaging with outside communities.

We had two Stand-in Schools, one in Berlin and one in Lisbon, they were quite different from each other. In Berlin we had a dedicated space, whereas in Lisbon we had no fixed venue and everything happened across different locations around the city. For me, Berlin was a more special experience. I think it was more successful as a school: it ran much longer and many people got involved.

What was the experience like, and how did the process of students organizing it themselves look in practice?

ATIn Berlin we started with an open call for participants, and people sent in proposals for workshops and lectures. We went through the proposals, organized them thematically across six weeks, each week had its own theme, and built out a calendar. Themes included Text and Language (which was my week), Disobedience and Society, Body and Labour, and others. Each week was assigned to two students, who communicated with that week's participants, wrote a short description, set the schedule, and organized the program.

What did you ask for in the open call?

ATThe brief asked for something related to alternative education in graphic design, art, or alternative research and practice. We specifically didn't want professional teachers to apply. Most submissions ended up coming from graphic designers, students, and multidisciplinary artists.

All kinds of people came through, and there was a huge variety of events and workshops. There was often a micro-cinema at the end of each week. A few of my classmates created tours and workshops specifically centered on the neighborhood, which was a Wedding district. There were a lot of site-specific things happening in Berlin. One moment I remember well: someone organized a ride on the Ringbahn, we did a full loop and did various things along the way.

Overall, Stand-in School, especially Berlin, involved a lot of organizing; we didn't do much graphic design as such.

When you arrived in Berlin, how much time did you have to organize everything?

ATIf I'm not mistaken, about a week and a half. We started before arriving, but most of it happened within that week and a half, and it was quite stressful. Hopscotch Berlin was a friend of our program and we got the space through them, which we shared with the Miss Read book fair. It was a very bare space, and the first thing we did was build furniture together with Manuel Rieder and set up the space, which was actually a lot of fun.

The Lisbon program was mostly built around location. How did you make those decisions?

ATFrom the start, it was decided that Lisbon would have a smaller scope. That year, the first option was actually Tbilisi — but given how the situation had deteriorated there, EKA also recommended we not go, so we decided on Lisbon instead. The main connection there was the Provisional School of Nothing, a summer school run by two people: Marco, a graphic designer, and his wife, a dancer and choreographer. It's very performance-based, and true to its name, very open and unstructured school. It made up a large part of the trip.

When we arrived, we first went with them to the village where the school usually takes place, had a workshop there together, and then returned to Lisbon. We had one week to plan: each of us had to choose a specific spot in the city and figure out what we'd do there — how the work relates to the space, or doesn't. Finally we came up with a packed one-week program where we moved around the city from place to place.

How did the city of Lisbon influence the themes and topics you chose?

ATIt did have an influence, though I don't think we arrived with the mindset that Lisbon specifically meant something. Often the location isn't chosen for conceptual reasons — Berlin was chosen because there was a community we knew. We went to Lisbon because of the Provisional School of Nothing, but once we were there, the place started to influence the process.

For me personally, the city and its urban spaces had a very direct influence. I'd already been working on themes of ruins and dysfunctional objects, and I wanted to design a game that could be played in the street. That didn't quite work out, but I found a specific spot: a staircase with ruins right beside it. The idea became to do something there, and from that point the whole process was about figuring out what I could do in that space and how to adapt to it. In the end, I made drawings which I hung around the space, trying to create a kind of wayfinding, but in a very subtle, non-designed way. I wanted the drawings to guide people from one point to the next. It was a speculative idea — most people actually ended up staying in one place once they got to the stairs. I also gave directions to some people without telling others, asking them to do specific things, as a way of subtly influencing the group.

Two of my classmates did something in Lisbon connected to a tradition of street libraries, books attached to trees, and they made a reenactment of it. We'd actually started making book pouches together in Berlin, and they took it further in Lisbon. They produced the pouches and organized events where we'd go to places where these street libraries used to exist, hang books there, and spend time reading while they told us the history behind these libraries.

Another classmate did something around Fernando Pessoa — the Portuguese writer who had an enormous number of pseudonyms. At the event, you had to invent your own pseudonym, but to do it you had to take a trip around the city and the neighborhood, and then write a letter to yourself.

Something else interesting was getting to know local practices and designers. There's a studio that makes posters for screenings, mostly art videos shown in a church that has been converted into a theatre. It has actually been running for a very long time. Their method is interesting: they have a base image that stays the same across all posters, and it's about printing over it in different ways each time.

One workshop was also held in a bar — and that was it, we just had drinks.

Who was the audience in general, did locals participate?

ATIn Berlin the audience was very diverse, mostly designers and illustrators, but also people who came specifically to run a workshop, or who were passing through the city and joined us. There was also an entire group of Rietveld students who came.

In that sense Berlin was more successful, because in Lisbon we had less engagement from the local public, probably because it was more spontaneous and shorter. There were participants, but I think around ten people were there consistently. And on one occasion Marco brought his students along. I had a feeling Lisbon was more focused on us, with more of us running the workshops ourselves, which I think naturally reduces the outside audience. A lot of the audience in Berlin were friends of people who were running the workshops.

During this nomadic learning experience, what did you gain that a traditional classroom couldn't offer?

ATAlmost everything I've been describing is inherently something you can't do in a traditional classroom — though depending on how you define "traditional classroom," there's also a lot you can do there.

When you travel, you absorb so much, and get to see all these practices that exist elsewhere. It's similar to what comes with any kind of challenge, you try to absorb it and channel it into work in a more tangible way. You also have to collaborate a lot; it's very collective, and things come out of it that wouldn't emerge in a classroom setting.

There was always an interaction with urban and physical spaces whenever we were making something. For me, Stand-in School was about learning how to organize, and being in the role of host, mediator, and curator. It feels like a real thing, it breaks out of the classroom and actually happens. When you're in an urban setting like in Lisbon, it's a completely different experience: you're turning the city itself into a classroom and engaging with the public directly. I do think that for a designer, being comfortable having an audience — in a very direct, "people are watching you" way — is a genuinely valuable skill.

How do you imagine what will be the role of design education in the future? Do you think there will be a need for architecture?

ATI don't think I understand design as a discipline all that well. I've done some teaching in the MA, but I still don't feel like I have a solid grasp of education, its state or its role, historically or now. I don't see a clear role or a fixed form that design education needs to take.

Nomadic and alternative forms of education were at the core of how I was taught in this MA, and many times while studying I found myself asking: what does it mean for me as a designer to be approaching topics this way, trying to design in these alternative, even weird ways? I think having a classroom, a building, is good and should always remain relevant. In Berlin we had a space, and I think that space was really important, I wouldn't want to just do everything out in the streets. But it's about approaching education as an experience: everything you do, how you interact with a space, these are all teaching tools. Leaving the classroom and going outside also means something. Teaching is so dynamic, you need to reach a certain state of mind. It's really effective when you're working and then suddenly "now we're going outside" — that was also a big part of the teaching approach at EKA in Tallinn. It energizes you and puts you in a position to think differently. That shift in perception, that heightened awareness, your brain working in a new way — I think that's a big part of education and the process of learning. So I don't think classrooms will disappear.

As a whole, our MA tries to be very horizontal. We think about education a lot — we read about it, second-year MA students always run workshops for BA students, and so on. Personally, looking back, those two years were for me a study of perception itself: a study of the image, of image-making, of looking at things and then looking at things collectively, and of how our perception is reflected in representation. I also think Stand-in School, and these experiences more broadly, point to the plurality of practices and points of view. Maybe that's where I see the role of design education now — and of design itself. The challenge is how to design for a plural world.

When I went to study in Tallinn, I thought there was a design canon, a set of ideas. In Tbilisi, design is largely commodified, it's all about advertising, and honestly sometimes I don't even consider commercial advertising to be design. Then I got to Tallinn and everyone was criticizing modernism, and I thought: "what even is modernism in design?" I hadn't really been taught what its ideas actually were. The way I've come to understand it, modernism has a human goal: to make life better, to optimize things so we don't have to struggle as much. But it came from a European tradition and eventually hit walls. And I think that modernist drive toward standardization and optimization can be — and has been — commodified for far less human ends.

For me, design needs to hold onto that same underlying goal: putting lived experience at the center. As for the form design education should take, I don't think it needs a fixed shape. Not without classrooms, not only classrooms. It needs to be a balancing act.
