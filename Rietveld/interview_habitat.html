Can you tell us a bit about Habitat? We heard that you’re turning five years old this year, maybe you can tell us about that too.

ETIn 2021, the first group of the collective, who already knew each other, started together. We shared the idea of continuing to work on projects — enjoying and experimenting with publishing processes, events, and artistic practices; things we had been exploring in urban contexts as students. But we also wanted to reflect on our personal interests and bring these practices into mountain areas.

That desire led us to start looking for space, ideally a village where we could organize residency and find the environment we were curious about. Through a series of coincidences through the association we’re part of, we discovered the village Tredozio and got in touch with the space in Cà de Monti. From the moment we first saw it we felt it was exactly what we were looking for, a place that could host people, events, workshops and other creative activities, all within a special environment surrounded by mountains and woods. In Mid-July 2021, we launched our first residency, which brought together several collectives and creative people over four weeks. Each week, along with some extra moments, included public spots in three different villages in the surrounding area.

FPHabitat was supposed to last one month but then we never left. After a few months, some people started living here permanently. Over these five years, a lot has changed. We never stuck to a fixed manifesto or statement, we bend and change shapes quite easily. We’ve had different kinds of residencies and approaches. We’ve dwelled more in the house itself, but also increasingly engaged with the surrounding towns, especially our town. We started researching the area where we live, the Valle del Tramazzo, Tramazzo being the river that runs through Tredozio. Last year, for instance, we organized a festival centered around the river, exploring how we live with it. In recent years there have been quite a few weather-related disasters like floods, landslides, and these things touch everybody, not just us. It felt urgent to talk about it, to take an ecosystemic approach, even if we are not the team with and take an ecosystemic approach.

JLAs Poni was saying, it's a constant evolution. We’re constantly called to take the shape of different kinds of challenges, the administration and the curation of this place, alongside the events that ask something different to us every year. We are always finding new ways to sustain our activities supported by our cultural association and membership fees. We also continuously look for and apply for open calls and funding, even if it's sometimes a long uphill road. What keeps this place alive, at the end, is the time this group invests in it, treating it as part of their individual lives but with collective purposes. We bring together our interests, approaches and build, I think, a collective knowledge, a collective purpose and a collective action towards our shared objectives. The main aim from the beginning was to bring life to places that were quiet or overlooked, while cities were becoming overcrowded. The question was what can happen when you spend time together in a shared space, connect with the people and places around you, and try to build and make things as much as possible within the house, while also embracing the limits of being in the mountains. Year by year, the tools and infrastructure were updated, but the core question remained.

ETSo what we do now, Habitat is a non-profit association. We organize public events at Cà de Monti, in surrounding villages, or in nearby towns. As a collective, we give talks, run workshops, host residencies, and curate programs with university classes. We work on publishing from printed matter to digital infrastructures and creative outputs.

We want to know about the program you did last year with Gerrit Rietveld Academie. Could you tell us how it started and what the process was like?

JLEverything started a few years before with our first collaboration with the Sandberg Institute, it was an experiment with the second-year master’s students in the studio for Immediate Spaces. That was our first experience hosting a group of students together with a coordinator, organizing and curating a program of events and workshops in which both our and the students’ proposals were combined. Over the last few years, this kind of thing happened a few more times, it was a “passaparola” as we say in Italy, word of mouth. And the last group we had the pleasure of hosting was the graphic design students from Gerrit Rietveld Academie.

In this case, we started talking with the coordinator, Eva. The students organize an academic trip every year, and they proposed Habitat because they had seen other students from Sandberg doing this kind of thing with us. What we suggested was to not go directly in the graphic design direction, because the students are always with that all the time. So we proposed activities that weren’t directly correlated to their field. The radio was something we had first started with the studio for Immediate Space,and we reproposed the idea to these students, who were super excited about it.

ETThe theme we developed together, “Margin: Unset”, started as a computing and programming command with a specific function, and it's also part of our web practices. It's also a statement: let's unset margins. If we reach something related to graphics, it has to be through a completely opposite path. It also relates to the territory where Habitat is, in Italy, mountain areas and small villages are often called "marginal areas," which carries a somewhat negative connotation. So we said, let's start from this idea of margins, but flip it completely and do it in a totally different way. Each student was then asked to individually define their own content for the radio.

How long did the students spend in your space?

JLIt was one week, around 15 people.

ETThere was a collective organization throughout the days. At Habitat, we have our own roles and responsibilities to keep all the spaces and functions running. Some moments were structured, contents we proposed or developed with the coordinator and students. But there was also a lot of free time, in the sense that the students themselves were programming their own time and deciding what they were working on, all around the radio as a shared goal. They were inspired by the space, the reality around them, and made a lot of decisions from there. For our final output, we organized a proper online radio infrastructure and streaming on the last day, from 2 PM to 6 PM. It was a public event where anyone could join, with a bit of a party and a dinner.

JLThe radio allowed us to unset the margins between our specific time and moment, place, and an audience that wasn’t able to be physically there, but could still connect to the event throughout the radio.

FPThis brings up something we've been focusing on a lot lately — the term “publishing.” Not publishing in a strictly editorial sense, but publishing as the act of making things public. The radio is one example, but a dinner can also be “publishing” in this sense through its interaction with people. And what Jacopo mentioned, people can enjoy the radio here but also somewhere else, because it is a medium itself, it is broadcasting. The radio connects to something else that's been central to Habitat from the early days, the idea of being rooted in a place but also beyond it.

You’ve already worked with students from different programs. How was the experience of hosting a class and how does communication with students usually work?

FPThere’s always an initial content from the class, mainly from the students, and then we start a conversation in parallel with the coordinators as well, because it’s also a matter of finances, since the university pays a fee for the stay and facilities. From there, we try to develop in parallel what we can do together based on the class’s proposals and their desires.

In our experience, people coming from the city are already amazed to find that there’s another way of living, even if just for a week or for a day. From our perspective, it's great to have a class, because it's not just a group of people, it's a group that shares values, interests, and topics. Even if those topics aren’t exactly ours, when you bring them together with what we do here, something magical can happen. It’s a simple rule of dramaturgy: put two things together and something great will come out of it. Our skills, workshops, and just the daily life, the pure daily life of this place, combined with the development of a project, makes for something really special. We don't position ourselves as tutors. Maybe more like antennas — we try to transmit.

ETTo us, it’s been very interesting and nice when we have these occasions to work with classes. Usually these tend to come through reservations. Habitat and the people in the collective work with designers, architects, and critical researchers around narration and placemaking. So we always find occasions to expand and care about something we’re also researching ourselves.

In general, when we have people here, and especially when it’s a class, everything is shared. We have a big house where most guests have their own rooms, and the meals and duties are shared. You have to come together as one thing to make it all happen. Maybe we’ve been lucky, but as Federico was saying, it’s always been beautiful and very intense as a group of people. These kinds of programs, especially with the final public presentations, create an energy that brings 20 to 30 people together as one. “I'm cooking for the event because at 7PM we have an aperitif with a DJ set,” and this is really beautiful because you truly feel like you're working on something worthy and full. So I think this kind of occasion is precious to see people saying “I don't want to go back.” So, that says it all.

Since it's a rural territory and it’s not familiar for the students, were there any unexpected challenges or discoveries during the week?

FPWe saw people who were a bit in crisis, as was just mentioned, some said they didn't want to go back to the Netherlands anymore. Of course, it’s something that stays in the moment more as a joke, but, with Sandberg last year, people were drinking and walking around at night, and started questioning their life choices. But from my perspective, nobody really faced challenges in a negative sense.On the positive side, maybe it's a bit colder than city houses, and you get muddy going outside.

JLYou get very human things.

ETWe share so much with the people joining these programs, the living space, everything. We did feel a slight difference in life stage, because we’re around 30 olds on average, while the last class were bachelor's students. So maybe it wasn’t a challenge as such, but more about harmonizing and making sure everyone felt comfortable and happy being there. But it's also interesting that through our residencies and public programs, we really get in touch with the everyday aspects of life and the approaches to life of people from all over the world. That’s especially something when you're on the top of a mountain.

Many schools are incorporating nomadic approaches to education, like study trips, art residencies like yours. How do you think this way of learning influences student’s experiences?

JLI think it's a lot about the discovery and what discovery can bring each time. You are facing the unexpected, which forces a moment of reflection on how to recontextualize yourself in a new environment, or it can simply be the beauty of seeing the stars so brightly for the first time. So more than challenges, it’s about how you face simple things we’re no longer used to, like watching the stars. This kind of spontaneous environment, along with the themes and the people, allows you to think more about your place in that specific moment. You contextualize yourself and your work from a place, and bring it into a space that is somehow a bubble, but it is a bubble where every action resonates with the people around you and expands into something really interesting and powerful.

We saw students bring this collective way of doing things back home, trying to work more collaboratively on their return and on their university projects. I think it helps in realizing that you can do things more easily, spontaneously, and collectively. Rather than focusing on a specific objective, it’s more about taking care of the process, the people and the infrastructure that make it possible.

FPAs for a nomadic approach, we can only imagine how it could be. With different environments, the level of challenges would certainly increase, but again, in a positive sense too. Every time you go to a new place, it completely changes the ecosystem, not just in a natural environmental sense, but also in terms of how people living there would resonate with you. It's a matter of how your approach changes, and how being in one place shapes you in the next. Moving through different places probably gives a lot more experience than staying in one.

We didn't experience that ourselves, when we studied in Rotterdam, we never left the city where we were. On the other hand, the Dutch Art Institute is a fully nomadic school, which a friend of ours has really enjoyed. I think it is really valuable to travel through different situations and always interact with new people.

As a final question, do you think we still need architecture for the school in the future? How do you see the future education from this perspective?

ETFor me, it could be somewhere in the middle. I'm really interested, not just in learning processes, but in living processes in general — Having the possibility of being less permanent, moving more easily, living more fluidly. I believe in accessible spaces, whether old or new, that can function in multiple ways — both as places like Habitat and as university buildings. Spaces where you can actually do things in a certain way. We can have a wood workshop at Habitat, but I’d still love to have access to a proper wood workshop in a university setting.

Maybe what we need is redistribution of the contents, processes, and inhabitants. I believe in these semi-nomadic learning processes that can really get people in touch with things outside the university building. At the same time, I also believe in a university building that remains accessible and useful as a publishing tool, as a working tool, as an infrastructure.

FPIn contrast, more imaginatively, I'd like to think that the university of the future would be like the original university: a bunch of people under a tree and talking about things.

JLI think universities and the academic world, especially in Italy, need to be a bit deconstructed and be more flexible toward changes, new tools, new needs and new perspectives. Universities should look outside their institutional boxes more often. From what we’ve seen through our collaboration with universities, these kinds of programs and exchanges can be really helpful for both sides. People bring back home different approaches and ways of doing things, while we get to experience a growing network of people involved in what we do here. That's why I think universities should look beyond their institutional boxes and try to update themselves. In Italy, that process is quite slow.

FPIn our bachelor’s study in Italy, things were really stuck in the 90s. And even now, I'm teaching at the academy of Bologna, and after 10 years it still feels the same. So, I don't know, we could even position ourselves as a master’s course, as a university and it could really work well.
