Can you tell us about your study trip with Habitat — how was it integrated into your university schedule, and how did the process work?

VKIn the third year, there's a tradition that started about six or seven years ago. A teacher organized a school trip as a kind of residency, and it's been done every year since. The whole trip was proposed by us, and it serves as an internship replacement, because we don't have mentorship involved. Instead, we have the study trip with an applied assignment, where we do work for the school. Together, these two things kind of replace the internship. For us it started with a proposal from one student. We began discussing it last year. There were a couple of proposals — one was to go to France, to visit an independent publisher. The plan was to fly to Paris, stay at her place for a couple of nights, then fly back to Amsterdam. The other proposal was to go to Italy. Italy sounded more exciting, and that's why we went with it. We were 20 students and we had a specific project we needed to work on, so that was also a consideration..

What was the process and the communication with Habitat while organizing it?

VKWith material first: paper on the floor, pencils, tape, and a slow walk through the building before anyone spoke about outcomes.

Was there a moment that changed the pace of the work?

VKI'm not completely sure because I wasn’t part of the organization, but from what I heard, the process started with reaching out to a group from Sandberg, the master’s program affiliated with our school, who had visited Habitat the year before us and that's how we found out about them. We then contacted Habitat asking if a residency would be possible and shared our budget, the number of students, and so on. The head of the department and her assistant also got involved, coordinating with Habitat on how to structure the program. Though in the end, it wasn't quite as structured as planned, we got there and kind of did our own thing.

When you arrived there,how was the week structured, and what did the overall process look like?

VKThe first night we arrived and just settled in. Then the next day, around noon, we all sat together, they showed us some of their previous work and proposed the idea of making a radio. From there, it was just an open conversation about what we could do and what we wanted to experiment with. It was really nice that we were all sitting around this huge table together. And I feel like as soon as we heard about the radio, we were all pretty down for it. Everybody was super excited, so we stuck with that quite fast. Then of course, everybody wanted to try their own thing with sound.

Did anyone have prior experience working with sound?It's not a very common medium for graphic design students.

VKThere were a couple of people in our class who could produce music, but for the rest of us, not really. Habitat provided all the tools we needed, like microphones and whatever else, they had everything.

How was the process, were you working independently or in a group?

VKIn the beginning we were figuring out how to structure it. We had conversations among ourselves about what we might want to do, and then we all sat around the fireplace and started throwing ideas. We ended up with a list of around 30 ideas for little programs. Everyone was like an author and designer of their own segment. Some worked in groups, others individually, we also created a little visual identity using sticks we collected on a walk on the first day. Habitat was completely open, they were really curious to see how it would unfold, so they didn't overstep. They just gave us a safe and comfortable environment to work in. Of course, when it came to the technical side, they stepped in when needed. And Poni, one of the guys from Habitat, taught a few students how to code the website, and they all built it together. The whole thing was pretty organic, we didn't overthink the structure, it just happened. I think it had a lot to do with just being in the mountains, with nothing else to do and all these tools around you. It was really great.

You mentioned the environment. Were those 30 ideas inspired by the surroundings?

VKA hundred percent, yes. It was pretty much based around the location and whatever was happening at that time. There was a chicken coop, so me and a friend of mine read some texts to the chickens and recorded it. A lot of students recorded sounds from their walks or things happening around them and worked from there. Some students made sound collages. One friend had an incident with his eye and had to make a trip to the doctor, and he recorded the conversations from there. My friend Anya did a sound collage where she asked people to describe a sound in writing and then imitate it with their voice. I was given to the clinging of glasses, so I tried to recreate that with my voice, and she recorded everything and composed it all together really nicely. At the end there was also a communal playlist where everybody added a few songs, and it all played together. And we had an amazing party on the last day, there was a car in the backyard, a smoke machine and we played tag. It was really great!

So, you actually finished everything in that one week?

VKYes, we did, including the website.

Did anything challenging or unexpected come up along the way?

VKThe unexpected thing was that when somebody gets sick, everybody gets sick. I got sick too, though thankfully it was last night. Being up in the mountains with no easy access to medicine made it so much worse. I had a really bad sore throat and there was nothing I could do about it. It was awful. As working conditions, being outside wasn’t always ideal either. Sometimes your fingers would get cold, or there'd be too much sun on the screen and you couldn’t see anything. But in a way, you also want to put the laptop away and just be outside. Also, everybody had to cook and clean the kitchen twice. Two to four people would cook together for lunch and dinner. We had a schedule for who cooked and cleaned. And honestly the cooking and eating became a huge part of the trip, just it took so long to cook for 20 people.

During this experience, what was the difference compared to the class in your studio?

VKI think it was seeing everybody there and working on things at the same time, seeing how busy everybody was. That was really nice. Not that you don't see it at school, but it's rare to have 20 students together at once. Usually it's like 7 to 10 students overlapping.

As a final question, how do you imagine the future of design education? Do you think we will still need architecture for the future school?

VKWow, that's a big question. For me, architecture is important. It's really nice to have a studio space where you can get to work and share that with other people. Peers are a huge part of it. Being in the same place as all these motivated people at the same moment is really valuable. But I'm also extremely in favor of trips and getting outside of the bubble you start living in. You're only there for four or five years at most, and then you go out into the real world — hopefully having developed your own practice during that time, even if it was a somewhat isolated experience.
